This is the complete archive of Maison Margiela under Martin Margiela.

The story begins in 1989.

About

Martin Margiela was the creative director of Maison Margiela from 1989 to 2009,
establishing one of fashion's most influential and conceptually rigorous bodies of work. Unlike his contemporaries, Margiela rejected the cult of personality that dominated fashion, choosing instead to remain anonymous and let his work speak for itself. He never gave interviews or appeared for bows after shows, and no verified photograph of him exists from his time as creative director.



The Conceptual Foundation

Margiela doesn't follow trends or switch its identity. Concept comes before aesthetics. Starting from his very first show in S/S 1989, Margiela created a unique universe which repeated and developed specific motifs throughout his career. His work is characterized by:


Deconstruction and Reconstruction – garments turned inside-out to expose their construction; visible stitching, raw edges, and exposed linings; recycling and repurposing of vintage clothing; the celebration of imperfection and the beauty of decay


Anti-Marketing and Identity – blank white label secured with four visible pick stitches; all staff wearing identical white lab coats; communications via fax and anonymous interviews; the intentional absence of logo and branding


Technical Innovation – the iconic Tabi boot, splitting the toe like traditional Japanese workers' footwear; oversized proportions achieved through innovative pattern-cutting; experimental treatments of fabric: paint, mold, bleach; artisanal transformation of found objects into haute couture



Historical Context

After graduating from Antwerp's Royal Academy of Fine Arts in 1980, Margiela worked for Jean Paul Gaultier before founding his own house. He emerged during a pivotal moment in fashion history, alongside the Antwerp Six, offering a radical alternative to the excess of 1980s fashion. His first collection, shown in a Parisian playground with local children as accidental audience members, set the tone for his unconventional approach.



The Maison's Unique Identity

The house operated from an unmarked white building in Paris, with furniture painted in white paint that would gradually peel and age. This aesthetic of calculated imperfection extended to every aspect of the brand, with collections numbered from 0 to 23, each with specific concepts:


Line 0 – The artisanal collection, transforming found objects into haute couture; each piece is painstakingly handmade, blurring the line between art and fashion.
Line 1 – Women's collection; characterized by its timeless elegance and innovative tailoring, often emphasizing deconstruction and asymmetry.
Line 4 – A wardrobe of basics for women; offering elevated essentials with signature Margiela details such as exposed seams and unconventional cuts.
Line 6 – A wardrobe of basics for men; focused on versatile, durable, and elegantly minimalist menswear.
Line 10 – The classic wardrobe basics; a unisex collection emphasizing simplicity and functionality, inspired by the maison's minimalist ethos.
Line 11 – Accessories and footwear; iconic pieces include the Tabi boots, jewelry made from repurposed items, and bags with artisanal details.
Line 12 – Jewelry; conceptual pieces exploring unconventional materials and themes of transformation.
Line 13 – Objects and publications; encompassing home goods, artistic collaborations, and printed works that extend Margiela's vision beyond clothing.
Line 14 – Menswear collection; reflecting the maison's conceptual approach while offering tailoring with a subtle edge.
Line 22 – Shoes; including iconic designs like the Tabi boots and reinterpretations of traditional footwear styles.
Line 23 – Fragrance; introducing scents that reflect the maison's identity, such as the "Replica" line, evoking specific times and places through olfactory memory.



Legacy and Influence

Margiela's influence extends far beyond his own collections. His conceptual approaches have become fundamental to contemporary fashion:


– The elevation of vintage clothing to luxury status
– The questioning of fashion's obsession with newness
– The rise of deconstruction as a legitimate design technique
– The importance of concept over commercial trends


This archive serves as a comprehensive study of Martin Margiela's tenure at Maison Margiela, documenting a body of work that challenged and transformed our understanding of what fashion could be. Each piece in this catalog represents not just a garment, but an idea - a continuation of an ongoing dialogue about creation, consumption, and the nature of clothing itself.
